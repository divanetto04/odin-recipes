Carbonara

Description

This recipe will teach you how a nice plate of spaghetti alla carbonara is done in the Roman traditional way.

A bit of history

The carbonara's origins have been attributed to Cecilia Monti, a noblewoman from Polesine, who used to host meetings of the carboneria, a network of secret revolutionary societes.
However, it is also said that Roman people discovered the recipe from the Allied forces deployed on the Reinhard Line during WW2: they had the opportunity to taste the cacio e ova from Abruzzo and modified it by adding guanciale.

How it is done

Food serves the purpose of satisfying our hunger, and pleasing our palate. We won't assume there's a "historically correct" way of cooking this recipe, neither will we accuse anyone of blasphemy. Partisanship in kitchen is petulant and ultimately detrimental to cooking as a form of human expression.

Ingredients

Portioning for four people:

  • 400g pasta (aforementioned spaghetti, or mezze maniche, bucatini);
  • 200g Roman pecorino;
  • black pepper;
  • salt;
  • 280g guanciale;
  • 5 yolks.

Steps

  1. Boil a pot of water. You won't need much salt, as the pecorino is already very tasty. Add pasta to water.
  2. Get rid of the guanciale's rind, then cut it in thick strips. Let it simmer in a pan, on moderate flame, until the fat gets translucid. No oil needed.
  3. Take a bowl, add the yolks and mix with pecorino and black pepper. Add some of the guanciale's fat to make this mix creamy and dense.
  4. Keep some cooking water, then drain the pasta when it is al dente (a little hard on the teeth). Add pasta, the creamy mix and the cooking water in the guanciale's pan, and mix thoroughly.
  5. Decorate with some pecorino, strips of guanciale and some black pepper. Here it is! You carbonara is done, as they prepare it in old Roman osterias.
